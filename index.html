Chromosome Mutation

Krabbe disease is caused by mutations in the GALC gene on chromosome 14. These mutations lead to the faulty production of an important enzyme called GALC.

GALC Enzyme Deficiency

GALC enzyme normally helps break down certain fats. When GALC doesn't work properly, harmful substances start to build up in the nervous system.

What is Myelin?

Myelin is the protective coating around nerve cells that helps signals travel quickly. It's made from fats and proteins, including galactosylceramide and psychosine.

Psychosine Buildup Damages Myelin

Without GALC, psychosine builds up and becomes toxic. This causes the cells that make myelin to die, leading to loss of the protective layer.

Formation of Globoids

Dead cells and myelin debris form large clumps called globoids. These globoids disrupt nerve function even more, worsening symptoms.

Symptoms

Krabbe disease can begin at vastly different stages of life, affecting how symptoms appear and how quickly they progress. Early-onset Krabbe is severe and usually fatal by age two, with intense neurological symptoms that progress rapidly. Late-onset Krabbe develops later in life with a slower progression, though symptoms remain serious and debilitating.

Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplant (HSCT)

  • HSCT introduces healthy donor stem cells into the body
  • These cells travel to the brain and produce the missing GALC enzyme
  • Only effective before extensive damage occurs

Why HSCT Has Limits

How Do We Get It In?

For gene therapy to work, it has to reach the brain's cells. Scientists are testing five main delivery methods, each with unique pros and cons in how precisely ‚Äî and safely ‚Äî they can get viral vectors to the right place.

IV ‚Äì Intravenous

Into the vein

Arm with IV + brain outline

Simple injection into a vein. Low risk, but most vectors are blocked by the blood-brain barrier. Only a small fraction reaches the brain.

IT ‚Äì Intrathecal

Into spinal fluid

Spine with fluid path to brain

Injected into the cerebrospinal fluid via spinal tap. Reaches brain and spinal cord more effectively than IV. Moderately invasive.

IC ‚Äì Intracerebral

Into brain tissue

Brain with pinpoint injection

Direct injection into brain regions. Highly precise but invasive and surgical. Best for localized delivery.

ICV ‚Äì Intracerebroventricular

Into brain ventricles

Brain ventricles glowing with fluid

Injected into fluid-filled spaces inside the brain. Allows wide distribution during development stages.

ICM ‚Äì Intracisterna Magna

Base of brain fluid space

Delivered into a CSF pocket near the brainstem. Effective spread through cerebrospinal fluid; used in some animal studies.
